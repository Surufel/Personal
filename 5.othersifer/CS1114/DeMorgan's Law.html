DeMorgan's Law:
   "the negation of a conjunction is the disjunction of the negation of the terms"

Yes!

A conjunction (an and expression in python) :
           ( term1 and term2 )
The negation of a conjunction:
  not ( term1 and term2 )
The disjunction (an or expression in python) of the negation (not-ing in python) of the terms:
 ( not term1 ) or ( not term2 )
DeMorgan's Law (from boolean algebra)

In range:
LO <= n <= HI (in python)
In languages that do not have the chained relational ops (and this can be written in python also) :
(LO <= n) and (n <= HI)

Not in range:
take the expression for in range and not it:
not( (LO <= n) and (n <= HI) ) - note the parentheses
and apply DeMorgan's Law:
(not (LO <= n)) or ( not (n <= HI))
But this is silly!
(n < LO) or (n > HI) - note the < instead of <=)
And now it "reads" more like what you really mean in "not in range".

And can it just be:
(n < LO) or (n > HI) ?
If you can't remember the precedence of <, or, and >,
    then use parentheses
    (recall that parentheses are not operators, they are punctuators).
